Key Discoveries from This Interview

Discovery #1: Training Budget Shock ($9M for operators alone)

What We Learned: MES operator training will cost $9M (180 operators × $50K each), likely $12-15M total across all roles

Why It Matters: Original budget model had $300-500K for ALL training. Actual requirement is 2400-5000% higher. Training alone is 60-65% of total project budget.

Breakdown:

  • Classroom training: 2 weeks per production line
  • Hands-on practice: 3-month pilot on one line
  • Gradual rollout: ~2 months per line (2 weeks training + 6 weeks stabilization)
  • 24/7 support: 6 months post-rollout per line
  • Total per operator: $50K (training time + reduced productivity + support)

Impact: Budget increased from $5.5-9.5M to $16.5-23.5M, feasibility score dropped 5 points

Discovery #2: Sunday Window Math (Physical Timeline Constraint)

What We Learned: Only 48 hours per year available for system cutovers (Sunday 2-6 AM windows)

The Mathematics:

  • Available windows: Sunday 2:00 AM - 6:00 AM (4 hours)
  • Cannot use: 1st Sunday (preventive maintenance), 5th Sunday (extended maintenance)
  • Actual availability: ~12 Sundays per year × 4 hours = 48 hours/year
  • Systems to migrate: 8 critical systems
  • Time per system: 6 hours average (cutover + validation + rollback buffer)
  • Calculation: 8 systems × 6 hours = 48 hours minimum = 1 year perfect execution
  • Reality: 3-5 years accounting for parallel running, validation, issues

Why It Matters: This is a mathematical proof, not an estimate. The 18-month timeline is physically impossible.

Impact: Provided irrefutable evidence that timeline must be 36-60 months. Converted timeline from negotiable to physics.

Discovery #3: SAP Trauma as Organizational PTSD

What We Learned: 2018 SAP failure created lasting organizational trauma affecting this initiative

SAP Failure Details (From Maria):

  • Duration: 3 weeks of operational chaos
  • Production Impact: 50% production drop during chaos period
  • People Impact: 3 employees lost (1 quit during, 2 quit after), nervous breakdown of 1 person
  • Floor Impact: Operators working paper and pencil, entering data later, massive overtime
  • Trust Impact: 7 years later, "new system" = resume updates
  • Lasting Effect: "We're not doing that again" is universal floor response

Key Quote:

"When corporate says 'new system,' people update their resumes. That's the legacy of SAP. You're not fighting technology. You're fighting trauma." — Maria Rodriguez

Why It Matters: Cannot ignore or minimize SAP. Must explicitly acknowledge, demonstrate learning, and rebuild trust through partnership.

Impact: Added SAP trauma as CRITICAL RISK (20/25), identified need for explicit SAP retrospective and lessons learned documentation

Operator Capability Breakdown (Maria's Assessment)

Maria provided a realistic assessment of her 180 MES operators' technology readiness:

Group 1: Comfortable with Technology (1/3 of operators, ~60 people)

  • Younger operators (under 40)
  • Grew up with computers and smartphones
  • Will adapt quickly to new MES (2-4 weeks)
  • Actually prefer digital to paper
  • Can help train others

Group 2: Can Learn But Will Struggle (1/3 of operators, ~60 people)

  • Older operators (40-60)
  • Computer literate but not comfortable
  • Will adapt with proper training and support (6-12 weeks)
  • Need hands-on practice, not classroom lectures
  • Will reach competence but need ongoing support
  • Productivity drop of 30% for 2-3 months

Group 3: Will Struggle Significantly (1/3 of operators, ~60 people)

  • Senior operators (55+)
  • 25+ years at company, know machines perfectly
  • Limited computer experience ("hunt and peck" typing)
  • Will require extensive training and support (3-6 months to baseline)
  • Some may never reach full competence
  • Some may choose to retire rather than learn new system
  • Losing these operators = losing 25 years of machine knowledge

Critical Implication

Training must account for all three groups. Cannot assume "2-week training and you're done." Group 3 requires 3-6 months of intensive support. Budget must reflect this reality.

MES Migration Timeline (Maria's Breakdown)

Maria provided detailed timeline for MES migration based on her experience and operator capability assessment:

Phase 1: Design & Preparation (Months 1-3)

  • New MES system design and configuration
  • Training materials development
  • Trainer sourcing (manufacturing-experienced trainers, 4-6 month lead time)
  • Pilot line selection (Line 2, simplest products)
  • Volunteer operator recruitment

Phase 2: Pilot Program (Months 4-6)

  • Line 2 operators (20 people) classroom training (2 weeks)
  • Practice environment hands-on (2 weeks)
  • Parallel running (both old and new MES, 3 months)
  • Issue identification and resolution
  • Refinement of training materials
  • Success criteria: Line 2 at 95%+ productivity on new MES

Phase 3: Line 1 Rollout (Months 7-9)

  • Line 1 operators (60 people) training (2 weeks)
  • Cutover during Sunday window
  • 2 months hand-holding period (trainers on floor 24/7)
  • 4 weeks stabilization

Phase 4: Line 3 Rollout (Months 10-12)

  • Line 3 operators (100 people) training (2 weeks)
  • Cutover during Sunday window
  • 2 months hand-holding period
  • 4 weeks stabilization

Phase 5: Post-Rollout Support (Months 13-18)

  • 24/7 support team on-site
  • Refresher training for struggling operators
  • Workflow optimization
  • Documentation updates
  • Continuous improvement

Total Timeline: 18-24 months for MES alone (from design to stable operations)

⚠️ Critical: MES Must Be Migrated LAST

MES is highest-risk system ($10K+/minute downtime). All other systems (ERP, WMS, email, etc.) must be stable BEFORE attempting MES migration. Cannot risk production disruption while other systems are unstable.

Michael's Five Requirements (Per Maria)

Maria laid out five non-negotiable requirements for Michael to gain floor credibility and trust:

  1. Work a Floor Shift: 8 hours on 2nd or 3rd shift, actually doing the work (enter work orders, handle system issues at 2 AM, talk to 20-year veterans)
  2. Listen to Floor Input: Operators know the workflows, the workarounds, the edge cases. Design WITH them, not AT them.
  3. Commit to Training: Not 2-week classroom training. Real, comprehensive, hands-on training with ongoing support. Budget must reflect reality ($50K per operator).
  4. No Layoffs: Explicit commitment in writing. Some operators may retire, but no forced layoffs due to automation/efficiency.
  5. Acknowledge SAP: Don't pretend it didn't happen. Document what went wrong, what's different this time, how you'll avoid repeating mistakes.

Maria's Bottom Line

"We're not the enemy. We're not resisting change because we're stubborn or lazy or afraid. We're cautious because we've been burned before. We're protective because these systems are critical to our jobs and to the company's success."

"If you partner with us—really partner, not just pretend to involve us—we'll make this work. My operators are smart, capable, adaptable people. Give them real training, real support, and real respect, and they'll deliver."

"But if you treat this like a technical project instead of a people project, it will fail. The technology might work fine, but the people won't adopt it, and you'll have spent millions for nothing."
